## 1. Purpose of the Exercise Library

The Exercise Library exists to:
- classify movement categories
- support conditional therapeutic orientation
- maintain non-prescriptive, non-corrective language
- ensure coherence with the Unified Prescription Rules

It does not provide instructions or techniques.

---
## 2. Exercise Categories

The official categories are:
- grounding movements
- centring movements
- softening movements
- stabilising movements
- lengthening movements
- breathing-based movements
- gentle mobility
- gentle expansion
- gentle compression
- slow rhythmic movement

These categories describe qualities, not techniques.

---
## 3. Structural Intention Categories

Structural intentions include:
- increasing presence in the base
- reducing upper effort
- supporting midline coherence
- redistributing load
- encouraging balanced engagement
- supporting natural organisation

These intentions must remain descriptive and neutral.

---
## 4. Energetic Intention Categories

Energetic intentions include:
- supporting flow
- reducing stagnation
- softening accumulation
- reinforcing fragility
- containing dispersion
- grounding ascending tendencies
- lifting descending tendencies

## Flow of Grounding: Base, Support, Yielding

### 1. The Wave of the Arch
| | |
|-|-|
| **Objective** | Dissolve energetic rigidity where the body touches the earth, allowing the wave of grounding to rise without dams. |
| **Execution** | Standing, feet parallel. Slowly shift weight from toes to heels and then to the outer and inner edges of the feet. Continuous movement, like a wave. Free breathing. |
| **Duration** | 2–3 minutes continuous |
| **Frequency** | Once a day |
| **Ki Breath Movement** | Free and conscious breathing – the weight wave needs no command. |

### 2. The Invisible Writing
| | |
|-|-|
| **Objective** | Unblock emotional memory of old impacts, restoring fluidity to the base to write over forgetting. |
| **Execution** | Sitting, one leg crossed over the other. The free ankle draws capital letters in the air – from A to Z. Movement comes only from the ankle, not the knee or hip. |
| **Duration** | 1–2 repetitions of the alphabet per foot |
| **Frequency** | 1–2 times a day |
| **Ki Breath Movement** | Free, soft breathing – writing in the air requires no forced rhythm. |

### 3. The Rising Tide
| | |
|-|-|
| **Objective** | Tune breathing to the inner tide rising from the base, restoring trust in the invisible support of the ground. |
| **Execution** | Standing, feet hip‑width apart. Raise heels off the floor (inhale) and lower (exhale). Tide rhythm. |
| **Duration** | 3 sets × 10–15 repetitions |
| **Frequency** | 3–4 times a week |
| **Ki Breath Movement** | Inhale as you raise heels; exhale as you lower. Tide rhythm. |

### 4. The Continuous Circle
| | |
|-|-|
| **Objective** | Release excess containment that flattens contact with the earth, activating the circle that spins stagnant energy to the periphery. |
| **Execution** | Standing, transfer weight only to the toes of one foot, keeping the opposite heel on the floor. Alternate weight from one foot to the other, as if the toes were small wheels. |
| **Duration** | 2 sets × 10–15 transfers per side |
| **Frequency** | 1–2 times a day |
| **Ki Breath Movement** | Free breathing – the continuous circle keeps its own rhythm. |

### 5. The Breathing Root
| | |
|-|-|
| **Objective** | Restore connection with the earth as a dialogue of grasping and releasing, dissolving patterns of emotional uprootedness. |
| **Execution** | Barefoot, feet on a thin towel. The toes of both feet try to grasp the towel, pulling it gently back, then release. Grasping and releasing action. |
| **Duration** | 3 sets × 10–15 repetitions |
| **Frequency** | Once a day |
| **Ki Breath Movement** | Exhale as you grasp the towel (grasping); inhale as you release. The root breathes with the movement. |

### 6. The Gate of Yielding
| | |
|-|-|
| **Objective** | Open the inner space where silent yielding to life’s weight has installed itself, releasing collapse of surrender without defence. |
| **Execution** | Lying on back, one leg extended. The knee rotates gently outward (opens the gate) and inward (closes), keeping the foot relaxed. Movement comes from the thigh, not the ankle. |
| **Duration** | 3 sets × 10–15 repetitions per side |
| **Frequency** | 3 times a week |
| **Ki Breath Movement** | Free breathing – the gate opens and closes without hurry. |

### 7. The Slide of the Hara
| | |
|-|-|
| **Objective** | Harmonise the slide of the centre as a metaphor of controlled surrender, undoing rigidity of emotional resistance. |
| **Execution** | Standing, feet hip‑width apart. Transfer weight to one leg, while the other leg slides the foot forward, back, and sideways, keeping the heel on the floor. Upright torso. |
| **Duration** | 3 sets × 8–10 repetitions per direction |
| **Frequency** | 3 times a week |
| **Ki Breath Movement** | Exhale as you slide the foot in any direction; inhale as you return to centre. |

### 8. The Tripod of Support
| | |
|-|-|
| **Objective** | Reinforce the three support points that sustain the centre, converting weakness of support into serene firmness. |
| **Execution** | Lying on side, legs extended. The upper leg rises slowly, keeping the foot parallel to the floor. Lift only 15–20 cm. Lower with control. |
| **Duration** | 3 sets × 12–15 repetitions per side |
| **Frequency** | 3–4 times a week |
| **Ki Breath Movement** | Free breathing – the tripod sustains without respiratory command. |

### 9. The Spring of Encounter
| | |
|-|-|
| **Objective** | Release tension accumulated at the meeting of the lower axes (where fear is stored) by compressing and expanding like an opening heart. |
| **Execution** | Lying on back, knees bent, feet supported. Squeeze a soft ball (or rolled towel) between the knees. Maintain constant pressure while making small pelvic tilts. |
| **Duration** | 3 sets × 10–15 repetitions (5 s squeeze) |
| **Frequency** | Once a day |
| **Ki Breath Movement** | Exhale as you compress the ball/towel; inhale as you release pressure. The spring that contracts and expands. |

### 10. The Swing of Flow
| | |
|-|-|
| **Objective** | Return to the free segment the pendulum swing that dissolves post‑traumatic rigidity, teaching the body to forget forced immobilisation. |
| **Execution** | Sitting on a high chair, legs swinging freely. The right leg swings forward and back like a pendulum, without using thigh muscles – only the movement of the tibia on the femur. Repeat with left. |
| **Duration** | 3 sets × 15–20 swings per leg |
| **Frequency** | 1–2 times a day |
| **Ki Breath Movement** | Free breathing – the pendulum swing needs no forced synchrony. |

## Center of Gravity: Central Axis and Stability

### 11. The Tipping Bowl
| | |
|-|-|
| **Objective** | Correct the projection or retraction of the centre as an expression of fear of the future or defensive withdrawal, restoring the bowl that receives flow effortlessly. |
| **Execution** | Lying on back, knees bent, feet supported. Tilt the pelvis slightly forward (increasing lumbar space) and then back (compressing it). Tiny movement – width of a finger. Breathing: inhale on posterior tilt, exhale on anterior tilt. |
| **Duration** | 3 sets × 10–15 slow repetitions |
| **Frequency** | 1–2 times a day |
| **Ki Breath Movement** | Inhale on posterior tilt; exhale on anterior tilt. |

### 12. The Swing of the Clock
| | |
|-|-|
| **Objective** | Release asymmetries of power and imbalances of support by travelling all the hours of the dial – a circular recognition of disharmony. |
| **Execution** | Lying on back, knees bent. Imagine the pelvis as the centre of a clock. 12 and 6 = anterior/posterior tilt; 3 and 9 = lateral tilt right/left. Travel the whole dial slowly in continuous circular motion. |
| **Duration** | 2–3 sets × 5–8 complete cycles |
| **Frequency** | Once a day |
| **Ki Breath Movement** | Free breathing – the circular path of the clock needs no command. |

### 13. The Opening and Closing Book
| | |
|-|-|
| **Objective** | Undo fixed twists of the centre (life stories that distorted the axis) by opening and closing the book of body memory. |
| **Execution** | Lying on side, knees slightly bent. Rotate the pelvis forward (open the book) and backward (close), without moving the shoulders. Hand on the hip feels the movement. |
| **Duration** | 3 sets × 8–10 repetitions per side |
| **Frequency** | 3 times a week |
| **Ki Breath Movement** | Exhale as you open the book (rotate pelvis forward); inhale as you close. |

### 14. The Submerged Anchor
| | |
|-|-|
| **Objective** | Correct the tendency to project the centre forward – falling into the future without support – by anchoring with translations that return presence. |
| **Execution** | Standing, feet shoulder‑width apart. Feel the weight descending through the spine to the pelvis. Move the pelvis slightly forward, back, left, and right, keeping the torso straight. Translation movement, not tilt. |
| **Duration** | 3 sets × 8–10 repetitions (each direction) |
| **Frequency** | Twice a week |
| **Ki Breath Movement** | Free breathing – the anchor’s translations are continuous and fluid. |

### 15. The Spiral of the Base
| | |
|-|-|
| **Objective** | Dissolve deep tensions where base traumas reside, drawing spirals that reorder energetic chaos. |

| **Duration** | 2–3 sets × 8–10 circles each direction |
| **Frequency** | Once a day |
| **Ki Breath Movement** | Free breathing – the spiral needs no respiratory synchrony. |

### 16. The Yawning Cat

|-|-|
| **Objective** | Dissolve the rigidity of the armour that protects vulnerability, in a yawn that rounds and sinks excess defence. |
| **Execution** | On hands and knees (quadruped). The lumbar spine curves gently down (sink) and then up (round). Slow movement guided by breath: inhale as you sink, exhale as you round. |
| **Duration** | 3 sets × 8–10 repetitions |
| **Frequency** | 1–2 times a day |

### 17. The Centre Swing

|-|-|
| **Objective** | Release unilateral overloads of responsibility, swinging weight from side to side as if sharing emotional load. |

### 18. The Small Bridge

# PART II – THERAPEUTIC EXERCISES

*Open Kinetic Chain · Closed Kinetic Chain · Neuromobilization*

## Neck (Cervical)

### Open Kinetic Chain

#### 1. Chin Tucks
| | |
|-|-|
| **Objective** | Activate deep neck flexors and correct forward head posture; reduce load on posterior cervical facets. |
| **Execution** | Standing or sitting with neutral spine. Slide the head horizontally backward, as if making a double chin, without tilting or flexing the neck. Hold 5 s. Return gently. Avoid elevating shoulders or retracting the jaw. |
| **Duration** | 10 reps × 5 s hold |
| **Frequency** | 2–3 times a day, especially after prolonged computer work |

#### 2. Deep Flexion in Supine
| | |
|-|-|
| **Objective** | Selectively strengthen deep cervical flexors (longus colli, longus capitis), primary stabilisers of the cervical spine. |
| **Execution** | Lying on back with knees bent. Perform a gentle chin tuck and lightly lift the head off the floor (only 1–2 cm), keeping the chin tucked. Hold 8–10 s. Lower slowly without dropping. Do not recruit sternocleidomastoid (no chin protrusion). |
| **Duration** | 3 sets × 8 reps × 8–10 s |
| **Frequency** | Once a day or every other day |

#### 3. Anterior Isometry
| | |
|-|-|
| **Objective** | Strengthen cervical flexors without joint movement; indicated in early post‑injury phases or with pain. |
| **Execution** | Sitting, spine erect. Place palm on forehead. Press the head against the hand, resisting without allowing any movement. Keep breathing regular. Force moderate (30–50% of max). |
| **Duration** | 3 sets × 10 s contraction, 5 s rest |
| **Frequency** | 1–2 times a day |

#### 4. Lateral Isometry
| | |
|-|-|
| **Objective** | Strengthen lateral flexors (scalenes, SCM, levator scapulae) bilaterally; stabilise the neck in the frontal plane. |
| **Execution** | Sitting, spine erect. Place palm against the temple. Press the head laterally against the hand, resisting without movement. Repeat both sides. Avoid ipsilateral shoulder elevation. |
| **Duration** | 3 sets × 10 s per side |
| **Frequency** | 1–2 times a day |

#### 5. Posterior Isometry
| | |
|-|-|
| **Objective** | Strengthen cervical extensors (semispinalis, splenius, upper trapezius) safely and controllably. |
| **Execution** | Sitting, spine erect. Interlace hands behind the occipital region. Press the head backward against the hands, resisting without allowing extension. Keep posture erect. |
| **Duration** | 3 sets × 10 s contraction |
| **Frequency** | 1–2 times a day |

#### 6. Cervical Extension with Band
| | |
|-|-|
| **Objective** | Strengthen cervical extensors with progressive resistance; improve muscular endurance and posterior postural control. |
| **Execution** | Sitting or standing, band placed on the forehead (or occiput depending on desired movement). Perform controlled cervical extension against band resistance. Return slowly to neutral. Keep scapulae depressed. |
| **Duration** | 3 sets × 12–15 reps |
| **Frequency** | 3 times a week |

#### 7. Cervical Rotation with Band
| | |
|-|-|
| **Objective** | Strengthen cervical rotators (contralateral SCM, ipsilateral splenius); improve rotational motor control. |
| **Execution** | Sitting, band fixed laterally at head height. Rotate the head against band resistance to 45–60°. Return slowly. Perform both sides. Avoid compensatory trunk rotation. |
| **Duration** | 3 sets × 12 reps per side |
| **Frequency** | 3 times a week |

#### 8. Shoulder Shrugs
| | |
|-|-|
| **Objective** | Strengthen upper trapezius and levator scapulae; improve muscular support of the cervical spine. |
| **Execution** | Standing or sitting, arms along body. Elevate shoulders toward the ears in a controlled manner. Hold 2 s at top and lower slowly. Can use light dumbbells to progress load. |
| **Duration** | 3 sets × 15 reps |
| **Frequency** | 3 times a week |

#### 9. Nodding (Yes Movement)
| | |
|-|-|
| **Objective** | Mobilise and activate the atlanto‑occipital joint; indicated in cervicogenic headache and suboccipital stiffness. |
| **Execution** | Lying on back or sitting. Perform small flexion/extension movements of the head on the neck (like nodding), as if saying “yes”. Movement occurs at C0‑C1. Maintain chin tuck during the exercise. Minimal, controlled amplitude. |
| **Duration** | 2 sets × 15–20 slow reps |
| **Frequency** | 1–2 times a day |

#### 10. Active Lateral Inclination
| | |
|-|-|
| **Objective** | Improve range of motion and lateral strength of the neck; mobilise facet joints and stretch scalenes and SCM. |
| **Execution** | Sitting, spine erect, shoulders relaxed. Tilt the ear toward the ipsilateral shoulder in a controlled manner, without elevating the shoulder. Return to centre. Alternate sides. Do not force maximum range if painful. |
| **Duration** | 2 sets × 10 reps per side |
| **Frequency** | 1–2 times a day |

### Closed Kinetic Chain

#### 1. Forehead Press on Wall
| | |
|-|-|
| **Objective** | Activate deep cervical flexors in closed chain; re‑educate neutral cervical posture against resistance. |
| **Execution** | Standing 30–40 cm from a wall. Rest forehead on the wall with neck in neutral position. Maintain gentle, constant pressure. Body slightly inclined forward. Avoid flexing or extending the neck. |
| **Duration** | 3 sets × 20–30 s |
| **Frequency** | 1–2 times a day |

#### 2. Occipital Press on Wall
| | |
|-|-|
| **Objective** | Strengthen deep cervical extensors in closed chain; stabilise the posterior cervical segment. |
| **Execution** | Leaning against a wall with the occipital region in contact. Legs slightly away from the wall. Gently press the head against the wall, keeping the chin slightly retracted. Avoid excessive extension. |
| **Duration** | 3 sets × 20–30 s |
| **Frequency** | 1–2 times a day |

#### 3. Lateral Press on Wall
| | |
|-|-|
| **Objective** | Strengthen lateral cervical stabilisers in closed chain; frontal plane control. |
| **Execution** | Standing next to a wall, rest the temporal region on the wall. Apply gentle lateral pressure with the head. Keep neck neutral without excessive tilt. Repeat both sides. |
| **Duration** | 3 sets × 20 s per side |
| **Frequency** | Once a day |

#### 4. Quadruped with Cervical Stabilisation
| | |
|-|-|
| **Objective** | Maintain neutral cervical alignment during limb movement; integrate cervical stability into the global kinetic chain. |
| **Execution** | On hands and knees (quadruped), spine neutral including neck (head aligned with trunk, gaze at floor). Perform alternating arm/leg extension (bird‑dog) keeping the neck still. Do not let the head drop or extend. |
| **Duration** | 3 sets × 8–10 reps per side |
| **Frequency** | 3 times a week |

#### 5. Gentle Cervical Bridge (Wall)
| | |
|-|-|
| **Objective** | Activate the posterior cervical chain in partial weight‑bearing; precursor to more advanced bridging exercises. |
| **Execution** | Standing inclined forward, rest the top of the head on the wall at a height slightly below eye level. Gradually transfer weight to the head while keeping the neck neutral. Body forms a diagonal line. Hold. |
| **Duration** | 3 sets × 15–20 s |
| **Frequency** | 3 times a week |

#### 6. High Plank with Cervical Retraction
| | |
|-|-|
| **Objective** | Integrate cervical control into global trunk stability; prevent dropping into cervical flexion/extension during upper limb loading. |
| **Execution** | Push‑up position (hands on floor, body in plank). Actively perform a chin tuck while keeping eyes directed at the floor. Hold the position for the set time without losing alignment. |
| **Duration** | 3 sets × 20–30 s |
| **Frequency** | 3 times a week |

#### 7. Wall Slide with Chin Tuck
| | |
|-|-|
| **Objective** | Combine shoulder mobility with cervical stabilisation; correct postural compensations of the neck during arm movement. |
| **Execution** | Standing back to wall, heels, buttocks and back in contact. Perform chin tuck and slide forearms up the wall (wall slide). Keep head in contact with the wall throughout. |
| **Duration** | 3 sets × 10–12 reps |
| **Frequency** | 3 times a week |

#### 8. Forearm Plank with Cervical Control
| | |
|-|-|
| **Objective** | Maintain neutral cervical alignment under prolonged isometric core load; strengthen neck‑trunk integration. |
| **Execution** | Forearm plank position. Keep head aligned with spine (gaze at floor, chin slightly retracted). Avoid head drop into flexion or extension. The neck should be a natural continuation of the spine. |
| **Duration** | 3 sets × 20–40 s |
| **Frequency** | 3 times a week |

#### 9. Chin Slide against Fixed Hand (on table)
| | |
|-|-|
| **Objective** | Re‑educate the chin tuck pattern with proprioceptive feedback; strengthen deep flexors with stable support. |
| **Execution** | Sitting at a table, rest forehead or chin gently on the palm of the hand resting on the table. Perform chin tuck by sliding the chin backward. The hand is only a reference point, not resistance. Repeat rhythmically. |
| **Duration** | 2–3 sets × 15 reps |
| **Frequency** | 1–2 times a day |

#### 10. Static Bear Crawl
| | |
|-|-|
| **Objective** | Challenge cervical and core stability with all four limbs loaded simultaneously; activate deep co‑contraction. |
| **Execution** | On hands and knees, hands under shoulders, knees directly under hips. Slightly lift knees off the floor (2–5 cm) and hold. Neutral neck, gaze at floor. Breathe normally. Do not let the neck drop or extend. |
| **Duration** | 3 sets × 15–20 s |
| **Frequency** | 3 times a week |

## Shoulders and Scapular Girdle

### Open Kinetic Chain

#### 1. Band Pull‑aparts
| | |
|-|-|
| **Objective** | Strengthen scapular retractors (rhomboids, middle/lower trapezius) and external rotators of the shoulder; counter forward shoulder posture. |
| **Execution** | Standing or sitting, hold band at shoulder width with arms extended forward (chest height). Pull the band outward and back until it touches the chest, squeezing the scapulae. Return slowly. Keep elbows slightly bent. |
| **Duration** | 3 sets × 15–20 reps |
| **Frequency** | 3–4 times a week |

#### 2. Face Pulls
| | |
|-|-|
| **Objective** | Strengthen middle/lower trapezius, rhomboids, and external rotators (infraspinatus, teres minor); improve glenohumeral health. |
| **Execution** | Band or cable at eye level. Hold ends with palms down. Pull toward the face rotating elbows outward and upward (external rotation) – wrists end near the ears. Return slowly. |
| **Duration** | 3 sets × 15 reps |
| **Frequency** | 3–4 times a week |

#### 3. Prone Y‑Raise
| | |
|-|-|
| **Objective** | Activate lower trapezius and inferior scapular stabilisers; correct scapular dysfunction in elevation. |
| **Execution** | Lying prone on an inclined surface or floor. Arms extended toward the ceiling forming a Y (approx. 135° from trunk). Thumbs pointing up. Lift arms as high as possible without trunk compensation. Lower slowly. |
| **Duration** | 3 sets × 12 reps |
| **Frequency** | 3 times a week |

#### 4. Prone T‑Raise
| | |
|-|-|
| **Objective** | Strengthen middle trapezius and rhomboids; improve horizontal scapular retraction. |
| **Execution** | Lying prone. Arms extended to the sides forming a T (90° from trunk). Thumbs pointing up. Lift arms keeping elbows extended. Focus on squeezing the scapulae. Lower slowly. |
| **Duration** | 3 sets × 12 reps |
| **Frequency** | 3 times a week |

#### 5. External Rotation with Band
| | |
|-|-|
| **Objective** | Strengthen external rotators (infraspinatus, teres minor); protect the rotator cuff in overhead activities. |
| **Execution** | Standing or sitting, elbow bent 90° close to body (use a towel between elbow and trunk if needed). Band fixed laterally. Rotate the forearm outward, moving the hand away from the body. Return slowly. Avoid moving the elbow away from the trunk. |
| **Duration** | 3 sets × 15 reps per side |
| **Frequency** | 3 times a week |

#### 6. Serratus Punch
| | |
|-|-|
| **Objective** | Activate and strengthen serratus anterior; improve scapular stability and prevent winging. |
| **Execution** | Lying on back or standing with light band/weight. Arm extended forward at shoulder height (90° flexion). From this position, push the arm forward, projecting the scapula into protraction (away from spine). Keep elbow extended. |
| **Duration** | 3 sets × 12–15 reps per side |
| **Frequency** | 3 times a week |

#### 7. W‑Raise
| | |
|-|-|
| **Objective** | Strengthen scapular retractors and external rotators in a combined pattern; improve posture and shoulder function. |
| **Execution** | Lying prone. Arms in a W shape (elbows bent 90°, arms away from body). Lift forearms off the floor by externally rotating the shoulders. Squeeze scapulae at the top. Lower slowly. |
| **Duration** | 3 sets × 12 reps |
| **Frequency** | 3 times a week |

#### 8. Lateral Raise
| | |
|-|-|
| **Objective** | Strengthen middle deltoid and supraspinatus; improve shoulder abduction strength. |
| **Execution** | Standing, dumbbells or band, arms along body. Raise arms laterally to shoulder height (90°), keeping elbows slightly bent and thumb slightly turned down (not up). Lower slowly in 2–3 s. |
| **Duration** | 3 sets × 12–15 reps |
| **Frequency** | 3 times a week |

#### 9. Scaption
| | |
|-|-|
| **Objective** | Strengthen anterior deltoid and supraspinatus in a biomechanically favourable angle; reduce subacromial impingement risk. |
| **Execution** | Standing, light dumbbells. Raise arms in a plane approximately 30° forward of the frontal plane (scapular plane), thumb pointing up. Raise to 90°. Do not exceed pain. Lower slowly. |
| **Duration** | 3 sets × 12–15 reps |
| **Frequency** | 3 times a week |

#### 10. Internal Rotation with Band
| | |
|-|-|
| **Objective** | Strengthen subscapularis; balance external/internal rotator ratio; relevant for overhead athletes. |
| **Execution** | Standing, band fixed laterally at elbow height. Elbow bent 90° close to trunk. Rotate the forearm inward (bringing hand toward abdomen). Return slowly. Avoid moving the elbow away. |
| **Duration** | 3 sets × 15 reps per side |
| **Frequency** | 3 times a week |

### Closed Kinetic Chain

#### 1. Scapular Push‑ups
| | |
|-|-|
| **Objective** | Activate and strengthen serratus anterior in closed chain; improve scapular stability and prevent winging. |
| **Execution** | High plank position (hands on floor, arms extended). Without bending the elbows, let the chest sink between the shoulders (scapular retraction). Then push the chest upward, protracting the scapulae maximally. Repeat. |
| **Duration** | 3 sets × 10–12 reps |
| **Frequency** | 3 times a week |

#### 2. High Plank
| | |
|-|-|
| **Objective** | Isometrically stabilise the scapular girdle; strengthen serratus anterior, pectorals, and core in an integrated manner. |
| **Execution** | Hands directly under shoulders, fingers spread. Body in a straight line from head to heels. Actively push the floor with the hands (light scapular protraction). Do not let scapulae settle – maintain activation. |
| **Duration** | 3 sets × 30–60 s |
| **Frequency** | 3–4 times a week |

#### 3. Shoulder Taps
| | |
|-|-|
| **Objective** | Strengthen dynamic stability of the supporting shoulder; challenge anti‑rotational core control. |
| **Execution** | High plank position. Lift one hand and tap the opposite shoulder. Replace. Alternate sides. Keep hips stable without rotation. Widen feet for a larger base initially. |
| **Duration** | 3 sets × 8–10 taps per side |
| **Frequency** | 3 times a week |

#### 4. Wall Slides
| | |
|-|-|
| **Objective** | Mobilise the shoulder in elevation with postural feedback from the wall; improve lower trapezius activation and upward scapular rotation. |
| **Execution** | Standing back to wall, feet slightly apart. Forearms in contact with wall, elbows at 90°. Slide arms upward keeping forearm contact with the wall. If lumbar spine leaves the wall, stop and reduce range. |
| **Duration** | 3 sets × 10–12 reps |
| **Frequency** | 3 times a week |

#### 5. Pike Push‑ups
| | |
|-|-|
| **Objective** | Strengthen deltoids and scapular girdle with partial bodyweight load; progression for overhead movements. |
| **Execution** | Inverted V position (hips elevated, body in triangle). Bend elbows lowering the head toward the floor between the hands. Push back to start. Scapulae should move in upward rotation. |
| **Duration** | 3 sets × 8–10 reps |
| **Frequency** | 3 times a week |

#### 6. Side Plank
| | |
|-|-|
| **Objective** | Isometrically strengthen the supporting shoulder; activate quadratus lumborum, obliques, and hip abductors. |
| **Execution** | Lying on side, support on forearm (elbow under shoulder) or palm. Lift hips forming a straight line. The supporting shoulder must be active (not sinking). Gaze forward. Breathe normally. |
| **Duration** | 3 sets × 20–40 s per side |
| **Frequency** | 3 times a week |

#### 7. Inchworms
| | |
|-|-|
| **Objective** | Integrate shoulder mobility with core stability; mobilise the posterior chain (hamstrings and spine). |
| **Execution** | Standing, bend forward and place hands on the floor. Walk the hands forward to a plank position. Perform a push‑up (optional). Walk the hands back to the feet. Stand up. Keep elbows extended during the “walk”. |
| **Duration** | 3 sets × 6–8 reps |
| **Frequency** | 3 times a week (may be used as warm‑up) |

#### 8. Bench Dips
| | |
|-|-|
| **Objective** | Strengthen triceps, anterior deltoid, and lower pectorals; work elbow extension with fixed hands. |
| **Execution** | Hands on a bench behind, fingers forward, elbows pointing back. Lower by bending elbows to 90° (or less if shoulder discomfort). Push back up. Avoid flaring elbows too wide. Legs extended for more difficulty. |
| **Duration** | 3 sets × 10–12 reps |
| **Frequency** | 3 times a week |

#### 9. Plank Reach
| | |
|-|-|
| **Objective** | Challenge scapular and core stability during unilateral reaching; strengthen serratus anterior dynamically. |
| **Execution** | High plank position. Extend one arm forward keeping the body stable. Return. Alternate sides. Keep hips level and avoid trunk rotation. Progress with wrist weights. |
| **Duration** | 3 sets × 8 reaches per side |
| **Frequency** | 3 times a week |

#### 10. Push‑ups
| | |
|-|-|
| **Objective** | Strengthen pectorals, triceps, and serratus anterior in closed chain; a complete functional exercise for the scapular girdle. |
| **Execution** | Hands slightly wider than shoulders, body in a straight line. Lower slowly (2–3 s) until chest nearly touches the floor. Push back explosively or controlled. Keep scapulae active (protraction at top). Modification: knees on floor. |
| **Duration** | 3 sets × 8–15 reps (according to capacity) |
| **Frequency** | 3–4 times a week |

## Spine (Thoracic and Lumbar)

### Open Kinetic Chain

#### 1. Dead Bug
| | |
|-|-|
| **Objective** | Activate deep core (transversus abdominis, multifidus) in a non‑weight‑bearing position; learn lumbopelvic control during limb movement. |
| **Execution** | Lying on back, arms extended toward ceiling, knees at 90° in the air. Keeping the lumbar spine glued to the floor (neutral zone), simultaneously extend the right arm overhead and the left leg toward the floor. Return. Alternate. Exhale during extension. |
| **Duration** | 3 sets × 8–10 reps per side |
| **Frequency** | 3–5 times a week |

#### 2. Bird‑Dog
| | |
|-|-|
| **Objective** | Strengthen spinal extensors and glutes simultaneously; improve balance and posterior chain coordination. |
| **Execution** | Quadruped, neutral spine. Extend the right arm forward and left leg back simultaneously. Hold 3–5 s. Return without letting the knee touch the floor and repeat on the opposite side. Do not lift the hip above the spinal level. |
| **Duration** | 3 sets × 10 reps per side |
| **Frequency** | 3–5 times a week |

#### 3. Cat‑Cow
| | |
|-|-|
| **Objective** | Segmentally mobilise the entire spine in flexion and extension; reduce morning stiffness and improve intervertebral disc nutrition. |
| **Execution** | Quadruped, neutral position. Cat: exhale and arch the spine upward (flexion), chin to chest, tailbone down. Cow: inhale and let the spine sink (extension), head and tailbone rise. Fluid, rhythmic movement, passing through each segment. |
| **Duration** | 2–3 sets × 10–15 reps |
| **Frequency** | 1–2 times a day (ideal upon waking) |

#### 4. Superman (Prone Lumbar Extension)
| | |
|-|-|
| **Objective** | Strengthen lumbar and thoracic extensors, glutes, and hamstrings; improve posterior chain endurance. |
| **Execution** | Lying prone, arms extended overhead. Simultaneously lift arms, chest, and legs off the floor. Hold 3–5 s. Lower slowly. Can progress with alternating unilateral lifts. Do not force excessive cervical extension. |
| **Duration** | 3 sets × 10–12 reps |
| **Frequency** | 3 times a week |

#### 5. Side‑lying Thoracic Rotation
| | |
|-|-|
| **Objective** | Improve rotational mobility of the thoracic spine; reduce lumbar and cervical compensation during trunk rotation. |
| **Execution** | Lying on side, hips and knees at 90° (fetal position). Arms extended forward, overlapping. Slide the top arm to the opposite side, opening the chest and rotating the thoracic spine. Hold 3 s. Lumbar and hips remain still. |
| **Duration** | 3 sets × 8–10 reps per side |
| **Frequency** | 1–2 times a day |

#### 6. Jefferson Curl
| | |
|-|-|
| **Objective** | Actively mobilise the entire posterior spinal chain in progressive flexion with controlled load; strengthen extensors in a lengthened position. |
| **Execution** | Standing on a step or box (optional), feet together. With light load (dumbbell or kettlebell), initiate flexion from the chin → neck → thorax → lumbar → hips (segment by segment). Lower to comfortable limit. Return in reverse order. **ONLY for populations without acute disc pathology.** |
| **Duration** | 3 sets × 5–8 very slow reps |
| **Frequency** | 1–2 times a week |

#### 7. Controlled Abdominal Crunch
| | |
|-|-|
| **Objective** | Strengthen superficial abdominal muscles (rectus abdominis) with control of neutral lumbar position. |
| **Execution** | Lying on back, knees bent. Hands behind head (without pulling the neck). Exhale and lift the shoulder blades off the floor through thoracic flexion. Lumbar spine remains neutral (not forcibly flattened). Lower slowly. Avoid neck crunching. |
| **Duration** | 3 sets × 15 reps |
| **Frequency** | 3–4 times a week |

#### 8. Dumbbell Side Bend
| | |
|-|-|
| **Objective** | Strengthen quadratus lumborum and obliques laterally; improve frontal plane trunk stability. |
| **Execution** | Standing, dumbbell in one hand. Laterally incline the torso toward the dumbbell side (lower the dumbbell down the thigh) in a controlled manner. Return to neutral by actively contracting the opposite side. Do not let the hip shift. Progress with load. |
| **Duration** | 3 sets × 12 reps per side |
| **Frequency** | 3 times a week |

#### 9. Pelvic Tilt
| | |
|-|-|
| **Objective** | Re‑educate motor control of pelvic position; activate deep abdominals and glutes; essential in early lumbar rehabilitation. |
| **Execution** | Lying on back, knees bent. Exhale and contract the abdomen, flattening the lumbar spine against the floor (retroversion). Hold 5 s. Inhale and return to neutral. Do not lift the buttocks off the floor. |
| **Duration** | 3 sets × 10–15 reps × 5 s |
| **Frequency** | 1–2 times a day (especially upon waking) |

#### 10. Windmill (Advanced)
| | |
|-|-|

### Closed Kinetic Chain

#### 1. Classic Plank
| | |
|-|-|
| **Objective** | Isometrically strengthen the entire core (transversus abdominis, obliques, multifidus, diaphragm, pelvic floor); improve anti‑extension endurance of the spine. |
| **Execution** | Support on forearms and toes. Body in a straight line (head‑spine‑heels). Actively contract abdominals and glutes. Breathe normally. Do not let hips rise or drop. Progress by reducing the base of support or adding movement. |

#### 2. Hip Thrust (Weighted Glute Bridge)

#### 3. Pallof Press
| | |
|-|-|
| **Objective** | Strengthen anti‑rotation core resistance; activate obliques and transversus abdominis with feet fixed on the floor. |

| **Duration** | 3 sets × 10–12 reps per side |
| **Frequency** | 3 times a week |

#### 4. Goblet Squat
| | |
|-|-|
| **Objective** | Strengthen lower limbs and stabilise the spine under anterior load; improve ankle mobility and squat posture. |

#### 5. Deadlift

| **Frequency** | 2–3 times a week |

#### 6. Reverse Plank
| | |
|-|-|
| **Objective** | Strengthen the posterior chain (glutes, hamstrings, spinal extensors); counterbalance anterior plank work. |
| **Execution** | Sitting on floor, legs extended, hands pointing toward feet (support on palms). Lift hips forming a straight line with the body. Contract glutes and abdominals. Head neutral. Hold. Can bend knees to facilitate. |
| **Duration** | 3 sets × 20–40 s |
| **Frequency** | 3 times a week |

#### 7. Hollow Hold
| | |
|-|-|
| **Objective** | Isometrically strengthen trunk flexors in a closed abdominal position; foundation of gymnastics and deep core control. |
| **Execution** | Lying on back. Press lumbar spine against the floor. Lift arms overhead and legs 15–30 cm off the floor forming a “banana” shape. Hold. If lumbar leaves the floor, raise legs higher. Breathe normally. |
| **Duration** | 3 sets × 15–30 s |
| **Frequency** | 3–4 times a week |

#### 8. Bear Crawl
| | |
|-|-|
| **Objective** | Dynamically strengthen core, shoulders, and hips in an integrated manner; improve contralateral coordination and spinal stability during movement. |
| **Execution** | Bear position (hands under shoulders, knees 2–5 cm off floor). Advance the right foot and left hand simultaneously. Then left foot and right hand. Keep knees low and spine neutral. Advance 10 m, return. |
| **Duration** | 3 sets × 10–20 m |
| **Frequency** | 3 times a week |

#### 9. Farmer’s Walk
| | |
|-|-|
| **Objective** | Strengthen grip, trapezius, spinal extensors, and core under bilateral or asymmetric load; high‑transfer functional exercise. |
| **Execution** | Hold a dumbbell or kettlebell in each hand. Neutral spine, scapulae depressed and retracted, chin retracted. Walk upright at a controlled pace. Avoid lateral leaning. Load should be challenging but allow posture maintenance. |
| **Duration** | 3–4 sets × 20–40 m |
| **Frequency** | 2–3 times a week |

#### 10. Suitcase Carry
| | |
|-|-|
| **Objective** | Strengthen anti‑lateral‑flexion core resistance; activate quadratus lumborum and obliques in a functional, asymmetric manner. |
| **Execution** | Hold a dumbbell or kettlebell in one hand only. Keep the spine perfectly vertical without leaning toward the loaded side. Walk at a controlled pace. The unloaded side works actively to keep the trunk erect. Alternate sides. |
| **Duration** | 3 sets × 20–30 m per side |
| **Frequency** | 2–3 times a week |

## Hips (Pelvic Complex)

### Open Kinetic Chain

#### 1. Clamshells
| | |
|-|-|
| **Objective** | Activate and strengthen gluteus medius and piriformis; improve pelvic stability and prevent knee valgus. |
| **Execution** | Lying on side, hips at 45°, knees at 90° (fetal position). Lift the top knee open like a clam, without rotating the pelvis. Hold 2 s at top. Lower slowly. Add a band above the knees for progression. |
| **Duration** | 3 sets × 15–20 reps per side |
| **Frequency** | 3–4 times a week |

#### 2. Side‑lying Leg Abduction
| | |
|-|-|
| **Objective** | Strengthen gluteus medius and tensor fasciae latae; essential for knee and low back injury prevention. |
| **Execution** | Lying on side, legs extended. Lift the upper leg laterally to 30–45°, keeping the foot in dorsiflexion (neutral) and without rotating the hip. Lower slowly. Use an ankle weight to progress. |
| **Duration** | 3 sets × 15 reps per side |
| **Frequency** | 3 times a week |

#### 3. Banded Hip Flexion
| | |
|-|-|
| **Objective** | Strengthen iliopsoas and rectus femoris; improve motor control of hip flexion. |
| **Execution** | Standing, band fixed behind (attached to ankle). Lift the knee toward the chest against band resistance. Keep spine neutral and do not lean backward. Lower slowly. |
| **Duration** | 3 sets × 12–15 reps per side |
| **Frequency** | 3 times a week |

#### 4. Quadruped Hip Extension (Kickback)
| | |
|-|-|
| **Objective** | Selectively activate gluteus maximus; isolate hip extension without lumbar compensation. |
| **Execution** | Quadruped, neutral spine. Lift one leg backward keeping the knee at 90°. Squeeze the glute at the top. Do not extend the lumbar (do not arch the back upward). Return without letting the knee touch the floor and repeat. Alternate sides. |
| **Duration** | 3 sets × 15 reps per side |
| **Frequency** | 3–4 times a week |

#### 5. Seated Hip Internal Rotation
| | |
|-|-|
| **Objective** | Strengthen internal rotators of the hip; correct external rotator dominance; useful in TFL and femoroacetabular syndromes. |
| **Execution** | Sitting on a chair, knees at 90°. Move the foot outward (internally rotating the femur) while knees stay together/aligned. Hold 2 s. Return. Perform both sides. |
| **Duration** | 3 sets × 12–15 reps per side |
| **Frequency** | 3 times a week |

#### 6. Leg Swings
| | |
|-|-|
| **Objective** | Improve dynamic hip mobility in sagittal and frontal planes; joint warm‑up and lubrication of the coxofemoral joint. |
| **Execution** | Standing supported on a wall with one hand. Swing the leg forward and back in a relaxed manner (sagittal). Then swing side to side (frontal). Gradually increase range. Keep trunk stable. 10–15 swings per plane per leg. |
| **Duration** | 2 sets × 10–15 swings per plane per side |
| **Frequency** | Daily as mobilisation/warm‑up |

#### 7. Side‑lying Adduction
| | |
|-|-|
| **Objective** | Strengthen hip adductors (longus, brevis, magnus, gracilis); balance medial and lateral hip musculature. |
| **Execution** | Lying on side, top leg bent with foot on the floor in front. Lift the bottom leg (extended) toward the ceiling. Hold 2 s. Lower slowly. Use an ankle weight for progression. |
| **Duration** | 3 sets × 12–15 reps per side |
| **Frequency** | 3 times a week |

#### 8. Extended Leg Hip Circles
| | |
|-|-|
| **Objective** | Improve motor control and stability during circular hip movement; activate deep stabilisers of the coxofemoral joint. |
| **Execution** | Lying on side or standing (with support). Slightly lift the leg and trace progressively larger circles with the foot, keeping the torso and pelvis still. Perform in both directions. |
| **Duration** | 2 sets × 10 circles each direction per side |
| **Frequency** | 1–2 times a day as mobilisation |

#### 9. Psoas March
| | |
|-|-|
| **Objective** | Activate iliopsoas controllably in supine; useful in early low back and hip rehabilitation. |
| **Execution** | Lying on back with band on feet (fixed below feet). Hips and knees at 90° in the air. Alternate extension of one leg while the other remains. Lumbar spine remains neutral throughout. |
| **Duration** | 3 sets × 10 reps per side |
| **Frequency** | 3 times a week |

#### 10. Frog Pumps
| | |
|-|-|
| **Objective** | Selectively activate gluteus maximus in an abducted, externally rotated position; alternative to bridges when knee limitation exists. |
| **Execution** | Lying on back, feet together, knees apart (frog position). Push the hips up through glute contraction and extension. Hold 2 s. Lower slowly. Total focus on glute contraction – no hamstring recruitment. |
| **Duration** | 3 sets × 15–20 reps |
| **Frequency** | 3–4 times a week |

| **Duration** | 3 sets × 8–10 lifts per side |
| **Frequency** | 3–4 times a week |

## Knees

### Open Kinetic Chain

#### 1. Seated Knee Extension (Chair Extension)
| | |
|-|-|
| **Objective** | Isolate and strengthen quadriceps; useful in early post‑surgical phases or when closed chain is contraindicated. |
| **Execution** | Sitting on a chair, adjust pad to ankle. From a flexed position, extend the knee fully. Hold 2 s. Lower slowly (eccentric focus). Avoid painful ranges. Control the arc of motion as prescribed. |
| **Duration** | 3 sets × 12–15 reps |
| **Frequency** | 3 times a week |

#### 2. Prone Leg Curl
| | |
|-|-|
| **Objective** | Isolate and strengthen hamstrings; fundamental for quadriceps/hamstring balance and ACL injury prevention. |
| **Execution** | Lying prone, legs extended. Curl the knee, bringing the heel toward the glute against resistance. Hold 2 s at top. Lower slowly. Do not lift the hips off the support. |
| **Duration** | 3 sets × 12 reps |
| **Frequency** | 3 times a week |

#### 3. Straight Leg Raise (SLR)
| | |
|-|-|
| **Objective** | Activate quadriceps and iliopsoas without joint loading at the knee; indicated in acute post‑operative phases. |
| **Execution** | Lying on back, one knee bent (foot on floor), the other leg extended. Contract the quadriceps of the extended leg. Raise the leg 30–45°. Hold 2 s. Lower slowly. Do not arch the lumbar. |
| **Duration** | 3 sets × 15 reps per side |
| **Frequency** | 1–2 times a day (acute phase); 3 times a week (maintenance) |

#### 4. Supine Terminal Knee Extension (TKE)
| | |
|-|-|
| **Objective** | Strengthen quadriceps in the last degrees of extension; essential in ACL recovery and knees with extension deficit. |
| **Execution** | Lying on back, roll/towel under the knee (20–30° flexion). Contract the quadriceps, extending the knee fully. Hold 5 s. Relax. Focus on maximal contraction in the last degrees. |
| **Duration** | 3 sets × 15 reps × 5 s |
| **Frequency** | 1–2 times a day |

#### 5. Quadriceps Isometry (with Towel)
| | |
|-|-|
| **Objective** | Activate quadriceps without joint movement; indicated in immobilisation phases or acute knee pain. |
| **Execution** | Lying or sitting with a rolled towel under the knee (slight flexion). Press the knee down against the towel, contracting the quadriceps. Hold 10 s. Relax. You will feel activation above the knee. |
| **Duration** | 3 sets × 10–15 reps × 10 s |
| **Frequency** | 1–2 times a day |

#### 6. Standing Knee Flexion with Band
| | |
|-|-|
| **Objective** | Strengthen hamstrings in open chain with progressive load; useful when a leg curl machine is unavailable. |
| **Execution** | Standing, band fixed to the ankle and to the floor/wall behind. Curl the knee, pulling the heel toward the glute against resistance. Hold 2 s. Lower slowly. Support on a wall if necessary. |
| **Duration** | 3 sets × 12–15 reps per side |
| **Frequency** | 3 times a week |

#### 7. Ball Adduction (Knee Squeeze)
| | |
|-|-|
| **Objective** | Activate and strengthen knee and thigh adductors; improve medial knee control. |
| **Execution** | Sitting, a medium‑sized ball between the knees. Squeeze the knees against the ball with moderate, sustained force. Hold 10 s. Relax. Can be done lying on back to modify. |
| **Duration** | 3 sets × 10 reps × 10 s |
| **Frequency** | 1–2 times a day |

#### 8. Leg Extensions with Ankle Weights
| | |
|-|-|
| **Objective** | Strengthen quadriceps at home without specialised equipment; accessible alternative to machine leg extensions. |
| **Execution** | Sitting on the edge of a chair, ankle weight on the ankle. Extend the knee to full extension. Hold 2 s. Lower slowly. Adjust weight according to tolerance. |
| **Duration** | 3 sets × 12–15 reps per side |
| **Frequency** | 3 times a week |

#### 9. Knee Circles (Hand‑assisted)
| | |
|-|-|
| **Objective** | Passively/actively mobilise the knee joint; lubricate the joint compartment and reduce stiffness. |
| **Execution** | Sitting or standing with support. Lift the knee to hip height and trace slow circles with the bent leg, supporting the knee with hands if needed. Perform in both directions. Progressive range. |
| **Duration** | 2 sets × 10 circles per direction per side |
| **Frequency** | Daily |

#### 10. Eccentric Leg Curl (Nordic Curl)
| | |
|-|-|
| **Objective** | Strengthen hamstrings eccentrically; indicated in hamstring tendinopathy and injury prevention. |
| **Execution** | Kneeling on a pad with feet held by a partner or under a sturdy object. Contract hamstrings and lower the torso very slowly (4–6 s) to the extended position. Use hands to assist during the concentric phase. Total focus on controlled descent. |
| **Duration** | 3 sets × 6–10 reps |
| **Frequency** | 2–3 times a week (not on consecutive days) |

#### 7. Squat on Unstable Surface
| | |
|-|-|

| **Frequency** | 3 times a week |

#### 8. Step‑up
| | |
|-|-|
| **Objective** | Strengthen quadriceps and glutes in a functional stepping pattern; improve lower limb strength and coordination. |
| **Execution** | In front of a step/bench. Place one foot on the step and push to step up with that leg to full extension. Lower controlably with the same leg. Alternate or perform sets per side. Vary height to progress. |
| **Duration** | 3 sets × 10–12 reps per side |
| **Frequency** | 3 times a week |

#### 9. Sumo Squat
| | |
|-|-|
| **Objective** | Strengthen adductors, quadriceps, and glutes; alternative to conventional squat for greater inner thigh activation. |
| **Execution** | Feet wider than shoulders, toes turned out (45°). Descend keeping torso erect and knees aligned with feet. Push through the feet to rise. May hold a kettlebell to progress. |
| **Duration** | 3 sets × 12–15 reps |
| **Frequency** | 3 times a week |

#### 10. Jump with Controlled Landing
| | |
|-|-|
| **Objective** | Train impact absorption and eccentric knee activation; prepare the lower limb for jumping/sports activities. |
| **Execution** | Two‑foot jump. On landing, bend the knees softly absorbing the impact (semi‑squat position). Knees should not pronate medially. Pause 2 s. Repeat. Start with low jumps and progress. |
| **Duration** | 3 sets × 8–10 landings |
| **Frequency** | Twice a week (return‑to‑sport phase) |

## Ankles and Feet

### Open Kinetic Chain

#### 1. Banded Dorsiflexion
| | |
|-|-|
| **Objective** | Strengthen tibialis anterior and dorsiflexors; improve dorsiflexion range essential for squatting and gait. |
| **Execution** | Sitting, leg extended, band attached to the foot (dorsum) and fixed forward. Pull the foot toward the shin (dorsiflexion) against resistance. Hold 2 s. Return slowly. |
| **Duration** | 3 sets × 15–20 reps per side |
| **Frequency** | 3–4 times a week |

#### 2. Banded Eversion
| | |
|-|-|
| **Objective** | Strengthen peroneus longus and brevis; fundamental for inversion ankle sprain prevention and rehabilitation. |
| **Execution** | Sitting, leg extended, band attached to the outer foot and fixed medially. Roll the foot outward (eversion) against resistance. Hold 2 s. Return slowly. Only the foot moves. |
| **Duration** | 3 sets × 15 reps per side |
| **Frequency** | 3–4 times a week |

#### 3. Banded Inversion
| | |
|-|-|
| **Objective** | Strengthen tibialis posterior and invertors; balance the ankle‑foot muscular complex. |
| **Execution** | Sitting, leg extended, band attached to the medial foot and fixed laterally. Roll the foot inward (inversion) against resistance. Hold 2 s. Return slowly. |
| **Duration** | 3 sets × 15 reps per side |
| **Frequency** | 3 times a week |

#### 4. Alphabet with the Foot
| | |
|-|-|
| **Objective** | Mobilise the ankle in all planes; reduce post‑sprain stiffness and improve joint proprioception. |
| **Execution** | Sitting, leg elevated. Trace all letters of the alphabet (A–Z) with the foot slowly and precisely. Movement must come from the ankle, not the leg. Perform both sides. |
| **Duration** | 1–2 repetitions of the full alphabet per side |
| **Frequency** | Daily (especially after acute sprain or upon waking) |

#### 5. Banded Plantarflexion
| | |
|-|-|
| **Objective** | Strengthen soleus and gastrocnemius in isolation; improve plantarflexion strength. |
| **Execution** | Sitting, leg extended, band attached to the sole of the foot. Push the foot down (plantarflexion) against resistance. Hold 2 s. Return slowly. |
| **Duration** | 3 sets × 15–20 reps per side |
| **Frequency** | 3 times a week |

#### 6. Towel Scrunches
| | |
|-|-|
| **Objective** | Strengthen intrinsic foot muscles; improve arch support and prevent plantar fasciitis. |
| **Execution** | Sitting, foot on a small towel on the floor. Scrunches the towel toward you using only the toes (without lifting the heel). Release and repeat. All toes should participate. |
| **Duration** | 3 sets × 10–15 reps per side |
| **Frequency** | 1–2 times a day |

#### 7. Toe Extension
| | |
|-|-|
| **Objective** | Mobilise and strengthen toe extensors; improve mobility of the plantar fascia and metatarsophalangeal joints. |
| **Execution** | Sitting, foot on floor. Lift only the toes keeping the arch and heel on the floor. Hold 3 s. Lower. Next, lift only the big toe keeping the others down. Alternate. |
| **Duration** | 2 sets × 10–15 reps per foot |
| **Frequency** | 1–2 times a day |

#### 8. Ankle Circles
| | |
|-|-|
| **Objective** | Lubricate the talocrural joint; reduce joint stiffness and improve global range of motion. |
| **Execution** | Sitting or lying, leg elevated. Perform slow, wide ankle rotations in both directions (clockwise and counter‑clockwise). Keep the leg still – only the ankle moves. |
| **Duration** | 2 sets × 10–15 circles per direction per side |
| **Frequency** | Daily |

#### 9. Point and Flex
| | |
|-|-|
| **Objective** | Actively mobilise the ankle in plantarflexion and dorsiflexion; warm‑up and joint mobilisation. |
| **Execution** | Sitting or lying, leg elevated. Alternate between pointing the toes (plantarflexion) and pulling the foot toward the shin (dorsiflexion) rhythmically. Go to maximum pain‑free range. |
| **Duration** | 2 sets × 20 reps per side |
| **Frequency** | Daily (as mobilisation) |

#### 10. Marble Pick‑ups
| | |
|-|-|
| **Objective** | Strengthen intrinsic foot muscles and improve toe dexterity and coordination. |
| **Execution** | Sitting, marbles (or paper balls) on the floor next to the foot. Pick up one marble with the toes and place it in a container. Repeat. Exercises intrinsic muscles and coordination. |
| **Duration** | 3–5 min per foot |

### Closed Kinetic Chain

#### 1. Calf Raises
| | |
|-|-|
| **Objective** | Strengthen soleus and gastrocnemius; fundamental in Achilles tendinopathy rehabilitation and ankle injuries. |
| **Execution** | Standing, heels on the edge of a step (or on floor). Rise onto the toes. Hold 2 s. Lower slowly (eccentric phase 3–4 s). For greater demand, perform single‑legged. Progress with load (backpack/dumbbells). |
| **Duration** | 3 sets × 15 reps (eccentric: 3–4 s descent) |
| **Frequency** | 3–4 times a week |

#### 2. Wall Tibialis Raise
| | |
|-|-|
| **Objective** | Strengthen tibialis anterior; indicated in prevention and treatment of shin splints (medial tibial stress syndrome). |
| **Execution** | Back to a wall, heels ~30 cm from the wall. Lift the toes as high as possible. Hold 2 s. Lower slowly. Heels remain fixed on the floor. |
| **Duration** | 3 sets × 15–20 reps |
| **Frequency** | 3–4 times a week |

#### 3. Ankle Lunge Stretch (Active Dorsiflexion)
| | |
|-|-|
| **Objective** | Actively improve dorsiflexion range; fundamental for squatting, gait, and sprain prevention. |
| **Execution** | Standing ~10 cm from a wall. Push the knee toward the wall keeping the heel on the floor. The knee should touch the wall. Measure distance progressively. Perform both sides. |
| **Duration** | 3 sets × 10 reps × 5 s per side |
| **Frequency** | Daily |

#### 4. Short Foot
| | |
|-|-|
| **Objective** | Activate intrinsic foot muscles and lift the arch; prevent and treat flat feet and plantar fasciitis. |
| **Execution** | Standing or sitting, foot on the floor. Without curling the toes, try to bring the head of the 1st metatarsal toward the heel, “shortening” the foot. Feel the arch activation. Hold 10 s. Relax. Progression: single‑leg stance. |
| **Duration** | 3 sets × 10 reps × 10 s |
| **Frequency** | 1–2 times a day |

#### 5. Heel Walking
| | |
|-|-|
| **Objective** | Strengthen tibialis anterior and dorsiflexors; activate the anterior leg chain. |
| **Execution** | Walk supporting only on the heels, toes lifted as high as possible. Walk 10–20 m. Keep trunk erect. |
| **Duration** | 3 sets × 10–20 m |
| **Frequency** | 3 times a week |

#### 6. Toe Walking
| | |
|-|-|
| **Objective** | Strengthen the triceps surae and improve ankle stability; activate the posterior leg chain. |
| **Execution** | Walk supporting only on the toes, heels elevated. Walk 10–20 m. Keep trunk erect and knees slightly extended. |
| **Duration** | 3 sets × 10–20 m |
| **Frequency** | 3 times a week |

#### 7. Single‑leg Stance
| | |
|-|-|
| **Objective** | Improve ankle stability and proprioception; foundation of post‑sprain rehabilitation. |
| **Execution** | Standing on one foot, eyes open. Maintain balance without support. Progression: close eyes; use unstable surface; perform simultaneous cognitive tasks. Keep the knee slightly bent. |
| **Duration** | 3 sets × 30–60 s per side |
| **Frequency** | Daily |

#### 8. Ankle Pogo Jumps (Low‑intensity Plyometrics)
| | |
|-|-|
| **Objective** | Develop ankle reactivity and elastic stiffness; indicated in advanced rehabilitation and return to sport. |
| **Execution** | Standing, jump rapidly on both feet minimising ground contact time. Movement should come from the ankle (not the knee). Keep knees semi‑extended. Start low intensity and progress. |
| **Duration** | 3 sets × 20–30 s |
| **Frequency** | 2–3 times a week (advanced phase) |

#### 9. Squat with Heels Elevated
| | |
|-|-|
| **Objective** | Improve dorsiflexion range and ankle mobility in the squat; increase quadriceps activation. |
| **Execution** | Standing with plates or a wedge under the heels (2–5 cm). Perform a full squat. Heel elevation compensates for limited dorsiflexion, allowing a more vertical squat. Good for mobilisation and strengthening. |
| **Duration** | 3 sets × 10–15 reps |
| **Frequency** | 3 times a week |

#### 10. Balance on Proprioceptive Disc
| | |
|-|-|
| **Objective** | Improve reflex ankle proprioception; fundamental for preventing recurrent sprains. |
| **Execution** | Standing on a proprioceptive disc, one or two feet. Maintain balance, trying to keep the disc level. Progress with eyes closed, external perturbations, or simultaneous motor tasks (catching a ball). |
| **Duration** | 3 sets × 30–60 s |
| **Frequency** | 3–4 times a week |

## Neuromobilization

#### 1. Median Nerve Glide
| | |
|-|-|
| **Objective** | Mobilise the median nerve along its path (neck → shoulder → elbow → wrist); reduce neural adhesions and improve nerve conduction in carpal tunnel syndrome and cervical neuropathies. |
| **Execution** | Sitting, spine erect. Arm extended laterally with elbow extended, wrist extended, fingers extended (stop signal position). Tilt the head to the OPPOSITE side and then return to neutral while slightly flexing the wrist. Coordinated, smooth movement. Can be done as a tensioner or slider. |
| **Duration** | 2–3 sets × 10 slow reps per side |
| **Frequency** | 1–2 times a day |

#### 2. Ulnar Nerve Glide
| | |
|-|-|
| **Objective** | Mobilise the ulnar nerve (elbow in cubitus valgus, wrist and little finger); indicated in ulnar neuropathy and thoracic outlet syndrome. |
| **Execution** | Sitting or standing. Raise the forearm, flexing the elbow maximally with the wrist in extension, fingers extended – as if placing the hand on the face (mask position). Alternate with elbow extension/wrist flexion and back. Slow, controlled movement. |
| **Duration** | 2–3 sets × 10 reps per side |
| **Frequency** | 1–2 times a day |

#### 3. Radial Nerve Glide
| | |
|-|-|
| **Objective** | Mobilise the radial nerve; indicated in lateral epicondylitis, radial tunnel syndrome, and dorsal forearm pain. |
| **Execution** | Standing, arm along body. Perform shoulder depression and internal rotation with elbow extended and wrist flexed (back of hand facing forward). Tilt the head to the opposite side. Alternate position. |
| **Duration** | 2–3 sets × 10 reps per side |
| **Frequency** | 1–2 times a day |

#### 4. Slump Flossing (Sciatic Nerve – Sitting)
| | |
|-|-|
| **Objective** | Mobilise the sciatic nerve and spinal dura in a sitting position; indicated in sciatica and lumbar radiating pain. |
| **Execution** | Sitting on the edge of a chair, slump position (trunk flexed, chin to chest). Alternate knee extension (straightening the leg) with neck extension (head back) in a coordinated manner. The nerve “glides” in two directions. Smooth movement – without pain. |
| **Duration** | 2–3 sets × 10 reps per side |
| **Frequency** | 1–2 times a day |

#### 5. SLR Flossing (Sciatic Nerve – Supine)
| | |
|-|-|
| **Objective** | Mobilise the sciatic nerve in supine; indicated in lumbar disc herniation with radiation and piriformis syndrome. |
| **Execution** | Lying on back. Raise the leg with the knee extended (straight leg raise). At the top of available range, alternate ankle dorsiflexion (tensions the nerve) with plantarflexion (releases). Perform slowly. Do not force painful ranges. |
| **Duration** | 2–3 sets × 10 reps per side |
| **Frequency** | 1–2 times a day |

#### 6. Femoral Nerve Glide
| | |
|-|-|
| **Objective** | Mobilise the femoral nerve and its branches; indicated in femoral neuropathy, meralgia paresthetica, and high lumbar disc herniation (L2‑L4). |
| **Execution** | Lying on side. Flex the top knee pulling the heel toward the glute (hip extension, knee flexion). Alternate with knee extension. May coordinate with contralateral cervical tilt. Smooth movement. |
| **Duration** | 2–3 sets × 10 reps per side |
| **Frequency** | 1–2 times a day |

#### 7. Fibular (Peroneal) Nerve Mobilisation
| | |
|-|-|
| **Objective** | Mobilise the fibular (peroneal) nerve; indicated in peroneal neuropathy, foot drop, and dorsum foot paresthesias. |
| **Execution** | Sitting, leg crossed. Combine foot inversion with plantarflexion. Coordinate with ipsilateral cervical flexion and contralateral extension. Slow, alternating movement between tension and relaxation. |
| **Duration** | 2–3 sets × 10 reps per side |
| **Frequency** | 1–2 times a day |

#### 8. Tibial Nerve Mobilisation
| | |
|-|-|
| **Objective** | Mobilise the tibial nerve along its course; indicated in tarsal tunnel syndrome, plantar fasciitis, and tibial neuropathy. |
| **Execution** | Sitting, leg extended. Combine dorsiflexion and eversion of the foot (maximal neural tension). Alternate with plantarflexion and inversion (relaxation). May coordinate with ipsilateral cervical flexion. |
| **Duration** | 2–3 sets × 10 reps per side |
| **Frequency** | 1–2 times a day |

#### 9. Obturator Nerve Glide
| | |
|-|-|
| **Objective** | Mobilise the obturator nerve; indicated in obturator neuropathy, obturator hernia, and medial thigh pain in athletes. |
| **Execution** | Lying on back. Alternate hip abduction (moving leg away) with adduction (bringing leg toward midline) passively or actively. Abduction tensions the nerve; adduction releases. Perform gently with comfortable ranges. |
| **Duration** | 2–3 sets × 10 reps per side |
| **Frequency** | 1–2 times a day |

#### 10. Breather’s Mobility (Neural‑Respiratory Coordination)
| | |
|-|-|
| **Objective** | Integrate diaphragmatic breathing with modulation of neural tension; reduce sympathetic nervous system facilitation; indicated in chronic pain syndromes. |
| **Execution** | Lying on back in a neutral position. Inhale deeply through the diaphragm (abdomen rises). On exhalation, gently assume a neural tension position (e.g., neck extension + leg extension). On inhalation, release and relax. Repeat coordinated. |
| **Duration** | 2–3 sets × 8–10 reps |
| **Frequency** | Once a day (preferably in the afternoon) |

## Prescription Rule – No Duplication of Movements

The Integrated Exercise Library contains exercises that, although classified as **Dynamic** (blockage release) or **Therapeutic** (physical recovery), may have **identical or very similar execution** (e.g., gentle spinal mobilisations, breathing movements, pendulum motions).

**It is not permitted to prescribe two exercises with the same execution simultaneously**, even if they belong to different categories. The clinician must choose **a single exercise per movement pattern**, prioritising the most appropriate approach for the patient’s goal:

- If the focus is **energetic/emotional release** → choose the exercise from **Part I (Dynamic)**.
- If the focus is **structural/muscular recovery** → choose the exercise from **Part II (Therapeutic)**.

Duplicating movements creates prescription overload, patient confusion, and risk of unnecessary fatigue. In case of doubt about equivalence between two exercises, the professional must verify the **step‑by‑step execution** (starting position, amplitude, rhythm, breathing) and not only the name or objective.

*This rule applies to all home exercise plans and must be observed during automated or manual selection.*
